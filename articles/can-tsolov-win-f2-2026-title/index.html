Can Nikola Tsolov win the F2 2026 title?

By Nikolay Tomov · Published 30 June 2026 · 7 min read

Short answer: yes. After six rounds, Nikola Tsolov is second in the FIA Formula 2 2026 championship, 2 points behind Gabriele Minì, with eight rounds still to run. He also has four wins to Minì's one. A driver that quick, that close, with that much of the season left, is a genuine title contender, not a hopeful outsider.

The longer answer is more interesting, because the reason he is behind at all is the same reason he might stay there. Here is the honest case.

Two points. That is the entire margin after six rounds, and it understates how strong Tsolov's season has been. He leads the grid on race wins with four — the Melbourne, Monaco and Spielberg Features, plus the Miami Sprint. Minì, the man in front, has won once, the Miami Feature. On raw pace, Tsolov has been the best driver in the field.

Why he is behind despite winning more

The gap exists because of two weekends.

At Miami, Tsolov won the Sprint, then retired from the Feature after first-lap contact. At Montréal, he ran fourth on the road in the Feature and lost the lot to a 10-second track-limits penalty that dropped him to twelfth. Two Features, close to zero points, in races where he was running where he needed to be.

Minì's season is the mirror image. One win, but points in every Feature except the Monaco round where a pit-stop failure wrecked his result. He has the higher floor; Tsolov has the higher ceiling. Through six rounds, the floor has been worth more than the ceiling — by exactly 2 points.

What it would take

The math is forgiving. A 2-point gap is nothing across eight rounds. To grasp how quickly it can move, look at Monaco: Tsolov scored 27 that weekend while Minì scored 6, a 21-point swing in one round. He does not need that again. He needs to keep finishing ahead of Minì by small margins and, more than anything, stop losing whole weekends.

Up to 39 points are available per weekend (pole, both wins, both fastest laps). Across eight rounds that is a large surplus relative to a 2-point gap. The title will not be decided by who is fastest — on that measure Tsolov already leads. It will be decided by who stops making mistakes that cost full weekends. The Montréal penalty and the Miami DNF are the kind of result Tsolov cannot afford again. Minì rarely produces them.

Spielberg showed the blueprint

The Red Bull Ring was a preview of how Tsolov wins this championship. He qualified third behind teammate Noel León, then explained his thinking in terms that sounded more like a points leader than a chaser:

I think there was no point for us to risk as much because, just looking at the championship, you need consistent results. — Nikola Tsolov, before the Spielberg Feature

Then he won anyway. A clean start put Campos 1-2 into Turn 1, he took the lead from León at Turn 3, and even a slow pit stop and a brush with Alex Dunne that broke part of his front wing did not stop him. He rebuilt the gap and crossed the line 3.3 seconds clear of Minì for his fourth win, the most controlled of the four. He out-scored Minì by 4 that weekend and trimmed the gap to two.

That is the version of Tsolov that wins the title: fastest in the race, but managing the weekend like the championship matters more than the highlight. His post-race radio caught the mood after a difficult Saturday:

The car was incredible all weekend and we bounced back from yesterday's loss. — Nikola Tsolov, after winning at the Red Bull Ring

What Minì is doing about it

Minì is not panicking, and his approach is the reason he still leads. Asked about the drivers closing on him, he kept it simple:

It does not really matter whether the people that are close to me in the championship win or not, because it's not in our control. The key is to maximise the result. — Gabriele Minì, Spielberg

That is the discipline Tsolov has to overcome. Minì does not need to beat Tsolov in a straight fight every weekend; he needs Tsolov to have one more Montréal. A driver who scores in every race is hard to overhaul, even by someone winning more often.

The Câmara wildcard

Rafael Câmara is the third name in this, even if he is a distance back. He is P3 on 82, which is 26 behind Minì and 24 behind Tsolov, after a strong recent run that included a heavy Barcelona points haul. Eight rounds and up to 39 points a weekend keep him mathematically alive. Practically, he needs both leaders to stumble repeatedly while he wins, and he has not won a race yet this season. Treat him as the driver who benefits most if Tsolov and Minì take points off each other, rather than a favourite in his own right.

The Honest Verdict

The title is his to win, and his to lose, in equal measure.

Tsolov has the pace — four wins from six rounds is the clearest evidence on the grid. Two points with eight rounds left is a lead that means almost nothing on its own. If he carries the Spielberg version of himself through the second half of the season, fast but disciplined, he is champion.

The risk is the same as the reason he is behind. One more zero-point weekend and Minì's relentless scoring does the rest. This is not a question of whether Tsolov is good enough. He has already answered that. It is a question of whether he can string eight clean rounds together against a rival who almost never has a bad one.

Frequently asked questions

How far behind is Tsolov in the F2 2026 championship?

After Round 6 at Spielberg, Tsolov is second on 106 points, 2 points behind leader Gabriele Minì on 108. Rafael Câmara is third on 82. Eight rounds remain, so the gap is small relative to the points still available.

How many races has Tsolov won in F2 2026?

Four — the most of any driver on the grid. He has won the Melbourne, Monaco and Spielberg Feature Races and the Miami Sprint Race. Minì, the championship leader, has one win (the Miami Feature).

Why is Tsolov behind if he has more wins?

Two weekends cost him the lead. He retired from the Miami Feature after first-lap contact, and at Montréal a 10-second track-limits penalty dropped him from fourth on the road to twelfth, scoring nothing. Minì has finished in the points in every Feature except Monaco. Tsolov has the higher ceiling; Minì has the higher floor.

What does Tsolov need to win the F2 2026 title?

Mainly consistency. The 2-point gap is closeable in a single strong weekend — at Monaco he out-scored Minì by 21 in one round. With eight rounds left, Tsolov needs to keep finishing ahead of Minì and, above all, avoid another zero-point weekend like Montréal. The pace is already there.

Can Rafael Câmara still win the F2 2026 title?

Mathematically yes, practically it is a long shot. Câmara is third on 82, which is 26 points behind Minì and 24 behind Tsolov. With eight rounds and up to 39 points available per weekend he is not out of it, but he needs the two leaders to drop points repeatedly while he wins consistently.
