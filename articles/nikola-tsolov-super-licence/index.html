Nikola Tsolov's Super Licence: how the 40 points add up.

By Nikolay Tomov · Published 28 June 2026 · 7 min read

One of the most common questions about Nikola Tsolov has nothing to do with whether a team will sign him. It is whether he is even allowed to race in Formula 1 yet. The answer runs through a points system most fans never see: the FIA Super Licence (spelled "Super License" in the US). The short version is that Tsolov is well on his way, and the popular idea that he has to win Formula 2 to qualify is wrong.

He is second in the 2026 F2 championship, and on the licence maths he needs far less than a title. Here is exactly how the points work, where his stand, and what he still needs.

What a Super Licence actually requires

No driver can start an F1 weekend without a Super Licence, and the headline condition is points. A driver has to collect 40 FIA Super Licence points across a rolling three-season window, awarded by finishing position in approved junior championships. Formula 2 pays the most; F3, F4 and the regional series pay progressively less.

There are other boxes: be at least 18, hold an International Grade A licence, complete roughly 80% of two seasons in qualifying series, pass an FIA theory exam, and log 300 km in a representative F1 car over two days. For a Red Bull junior leading an F2 campaign, those are formalities. Tsolov turned 18 in December 2024, so the age bar is long cleared. The 40 points are the part that actually gates eligibility, and the part people search for.

Where Tsolov's points stand

His biggest single haul came in 2025, when he finished runner-up in FIA Formula 3. Second place across an F3 season is worth 25 Super Licence points. His 2022 Spanish F4 title carried points too, but the three-season window has rolled on and those have now expired. So the number that counts today is 25.

That leaves 15 to find, and his 2026 Formula 2 season is where they come from.

The F2 maths

This is where the "top three or bust" myth falls apart. F2 is the most generous series on the licence table, and the top of it is flat — first, second and third are all worth the same.

A top-three finish in F2 is worth 40 on its own — an instant Super Licence, regardless of anything that came before. But Tsolov does not need that. With 25 already banked, fourth in F2 (30) takes him to 55, and even fifth (20) clears the bar at 45. The real requirement is simple: finish fifth or better in his rookie F2 season, and the licence is done.

The FP1 route

There is a second way to top up, and it is the one that makes even a modest F2 season enough. Every Formula 1 Friday practice session a driver completes is worth one Super Licence point, up to a maximum of ten. So a driver who finished sixth in F2 — 10 points, for 35 — would need only five F1 practice outings to reach 40. Red Bull juniors get those FP1 runs; that path is realistic, not theoretical.

Where he is right now

After Round 6 in Austria, Tsolov is second in the 2026 F2 standings on 106 points, two behind Gabriele Minì, with four race wins. A runner-up championship finish would be 40 licence points by itself. To put it plainly: on current form he is not scrapping to reach the threshold, he is on course to clear it comfortably, and most likely to bank a full 40 from this season alone.

So, is he eligible for F1?

The honest verdict

The licence is the easy part.

The points were never going to be the obstacle for a driver at Tsolov's level. Finish the 2026 F2 season anywhere in the top five — which he is currently well inside — and the FIA's 40-point box is ticked. Add the FP1 route and even a worse year would do it.
