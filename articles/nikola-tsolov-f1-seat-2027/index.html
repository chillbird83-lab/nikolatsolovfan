Could Nikola Tsolov get an F1 seat in 2027?

By Nikola Tsolov Fan — editorial · Published 25 May 2026 · 7 min read

After three rounds of the 2026 FIA Formula 2 season, the question won't go away. Nikola Tsolov is P3 in the championship with 35 points, 22 behind series leader Gabriele Minì. The Montréal weekend ended badly — a P4 on-the-road finish wiped out by a 10-second track-limits penalty — but the F1 conversation has not cooled. If anything, every junior-team profile from The Race to Beyond the Flag still names him as the favourite for the 2027 Racing Bulls seat.

So let's do this properly. What would actually have to happen for a Bulgarian to sit on an F1 grid for the first time? Here's the realistic case.

What the Red Bull grid looks like right now

Red Bull operates two F1 teams: Red Bull Racing at the top and Racing Bulls as the junior-team feeder. That gives them four seats and a structured pipeline that almost always rotates juniors through Racing Bulls before promotion.

The 2026 lineup as it stands:

Red Bull Family — 2026 grid

  • RBMax Verstappen — long-term deal, immovable
  • RBIsack Hadjar — promoted from Racing Bulls in his sophomore F1 season after a strong 2025 rookie year
  • RACLiam Lawson — third year at Racing Bulls; 12 points through 3 rounds 2026
  • RACArvid Lindblad — British rookie, signed Dec 2025; 4 points through 3 rounds, outpaced Lawson in pre-season testing
  • RESYuki Tsunoda — reserve driver for both Red Bull Racing and Racing Bulls

For Tsolov to enter the conversation, a seat among the bottom three has to open. Verstappen's isn't moving, and Hadjar — barely into a full season at the senior team — almost certainly isn't being demoted either. Realistically, that leaves the two Racing Bulls seats.

The realistic paths to a 2027 seat

There are three plausible routes. Each depends on results that haven't happened yet, but each is built on the actual 2026 lineup and the structural pressures Red Bull are already managing.

Scenario A — Lawson dropped after his third year

Lawson is in his third stint connected to the Racing Bulls seat. He's scored 12 points through three rounds in 2026 — solid but not commanding — and the rookie alongside him outpaced him in pre-season testing. If his second half of the season doesn't produce a clear step forward, Red Bull have a credible reason to make a change. A Lawson exit creates a vacancy that the highest-ranking junior outside F1 is positioned to take.

Most likely route

Scenario B — Lindblad doesn't convert

Lindblad has shown raw pace, but he's a rookie and rookies in the Red Bull system have historically been on a short leash. If the points return doesn't grow over the season — 4 points through 3 rounds is competitive but thin — Red Bull could move him to a development role and slot in an F2-graduated driver with a Super License already secured. That driver, if he finishes Top 3 in F2, is Tsolov.

Plausible

Scenario C — Tsolov directly to Red Bull Racing

The fan dream. With Hadjar in only his second F1 year and Verstappen contracted long-term, the path to the senior team is effectively closed for 2027. It would require Hadjar to be unexpectedly demoted, or Verstappen to make a surprise exit, plus an F2 title from Tsolov in the same window. The combination is possible but stacked.

Highly unlikely for 2027

The Marko factor

Helmut Marko officially retired as Red Bull's motorsport advisor at the end of 2025, but the relationship hasn't broken. Tsolov confirmed in May 2026 that Marko texted him personally to congratulate the Melbourne win — the same kind of direct line that historically signals continued interest in a junior's career.

This matters because seat decisions inside Red Bull are not made by committee. They're influenced by a small group of people, and Marko's voice has historically been one of the loudest, even from outside the formal structure. A retired Marko keeping in touch is not casual.

The first Bulgarian driver to win in F3 and the first to win in F2 is also the highest-ranked Red Bull junior currently outside F1. Marko isn't texting him by accident.

What Tsolov needs to do in the next 11 rounds

Pipeline talk and texting aside, F1 contracts are signed against results. Here's the realistic checklist for the rest of 2026:

1. Finish Top 3 in F2 — non-negotiable

A Top 3 F2 finish secures the full FIA Super License (40 points). Without it, the conversation can't happen. He is currently P3 with 11 rounds left — the gap to recover is 22 points, equivalent to slightly less than one full Feature Race win. It's still in his hands.

2. Convert race wins into a championship fight

Two wins in three rounds (Melbourne Feature, Miami Sprint) is the second-highest win count on the grid. The problem isn't pace. It's compounding bad weekends — the Miami Feature DNS (first-lap contact), the Montréal Sprint (P14), and the Montréal Feature penalty have stacked up to 23 points of unrealised result. He needs that conversion ratio to improve.

3. Pre-season F1 test

The standard route from F2 to F1 includes a Young Driver Test, ideally with a Red Bull Group car (RB or RAC). One was reportedly being discussed but had not been confirmed as of late 2025. Locking that test in — and not crashing it — is a prerequisite to any 2027 announcement.

What the F1 media are saying

Multiple independent 2027 grid predictions from mainstream F1 outlets have included Tsolov, almost always at Racing Bulls:

  • The Race — included Tsolov in an early 2027 grid projection at Racing Bulls.
  • Beyond the Flag — described Tsolov as one of the obvious choices for a 2027 Red Bull seat if his F2 form holds.
  • RaceValue — placed Tsolov at Racing Bulls in its 2027 silly-season prediction, alongside Lawson.

The mainstream F1 press doesn't usually flag F2 drivers this early unless the internal signals are there. It tells you the conversations inside Red Bull are real.

The honest verdict

Based on what's public right now:

  • Racing Bulls 2027: Realistic if Tsolov finishes Top 3 in F2 and either Lawson or Lindblad's seat opens. Probability is meaningful — not certain, but the most-discussed outcome in the F1 media.
  • Reserve / Test driver role 2027: The fallback if no seat opens. Equally plausible and almost expected given Tsunoda has held that exact role in 2026.
  • Red Bull Racing 2027: Long-odds — Hadjar is in only his second F1 season at the senior team and Verstappen is contracted long-term. Requires unusual circumstances plus an F2 title.

The first Bulgarian Formula 1 driver in history is no longer a hypothetical conversation. It's a specific person, in a specific car, with a specific 11-round championship campaign to finish. The path is narrower than it looked after Melbourne — but it has not closed.
