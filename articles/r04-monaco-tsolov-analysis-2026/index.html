Race Analysis · Round 4

From −22 to −1: how Monaco rewrote the title fight.

By Nikolay Tomov · Published 7 June 2026 · 8 min read

The defining image of Round 4 was framed in parc fermé. Nikola Tsolov stood on top of his Campos #6, fists raised, the Bulgarian tricolor pushed against the Louis Vuitton hoardings behind him. Twenty metres up the road, Rafael Câmara's Invicta sat parked against the Sainte Dévote barriers with a broken front suspension and a season-defining hole in his championship campaign.

Those two pictures told the whole story of the weekend, but only one of them told the story of the championship. Heading into Monaco, Tsolov was P3, 22 points behind Gabriele Minì. Heading out, he is P2, one point behind. A 21-point swing in a single round.

Tsolov also leads the entire grid on race wins with three. Câmara, who arrived as the closest rookie title rival, has dropped five places and is now 25 points behind the Bulgarian on his own rookie hunt.

The Sprint: calculated restraint and a bonus point

Saturday's Sprint was Campos Racing's first act of the weekend, and Noel León delivered it. Starting from reverse-grid pole after Friday qualifying, the Mexican rookie defended cleanly into Turn 1 from Roman Bilinski, who locked up, and led every lap of the 30-lap race. He brought home his second Sprint win of the season under track temperatures north of 30°C, finishing 3.1 seconds clear.

Tsolov's Sprint was a different exercise. Starting P9 after qualifying P2 overall on Friday, he found himself at the front of an unbreakable DRS train running from P10 to P18. At any other circuit, his pace would have cleared a path through; at Monaco it just queues you up behind the next car. He played the long game instead. On Lap 22, with the leaders' tyres cooked and his own car still healthy for Sunday, he created clean air, cooled his tyres, and on Lap 23 unleashed a qualifying-style lap to take the Sprint fastest lap. He crossed the line P8 — one point for the FL bonus, no car damage, and the front-row Feature start unspoiled.

It is the most overlooked single point on the F2 grid this season. Without it, the championship gap to Minì on Sunday night would have been two, not one.

The Feature: the tyre call that broke Câmara

Sunday's 42-lap Feature was billed as a head-to-head between the grid's two most highly-rated rookies. Câmara had taken overall pole on Friday with a 1:20.923 in Group A, recovering from a steering-arm contact with the barriers earlier in his session. Tsolov took Group B pole with 1:21.053 to lock in the front-row Feature start. They started side by side under clear, warm skies.

Câmara converted at the lights. Tsolov shadowed him for thirty laps. Through the first stint on the Soft prime tyre, the two pulled 2.5 seconds clear of Dunne by Lap 14, but Câmara could not slow down to manage his rears — Tsolov sat half a second behind through every braking zone. By Lap 30, Invicta's pit wall had a choice and no good answer to it.

The race-defining sequence ran from Lap 32 to Lap 35. Campos pitted first, taking Tsolov in on Lap 32 for Supersofts. Invicta covered immediately, calling Câmara in on Lap 33. The Invicta stop was clean and fast; Câmara exited the pit lane just ahead.

The difference was on the out-lap. F2 prohibits tyre warmers. Câmara emerged on cold Supersofts and had no front-end grip; Tsolov, already a lap into his second stint, had warmed his rubber and was at full racing pace. The DRS gap closed on the run to Turn 1. Side by side into Sainte Dévote, Tsolov on the inside, Câmara defending on tyres that wouldn't take it.

Câmara locked his fronts, skated over the kerbs, and crashed into the Sainte Dévote barriers. The race-leading Invicta was out. The Virtual Safety Car came out. The championship picture changed.

Behind the win, Alex Dunne's P2 was a quietly excellent drive — straight-up race pace from grid to flag, no errors, his second consecutive Feature podium. Beganovic's P3 was the gem: he ran a thermal advantage in the final laps, came out of the tunnel on warm rubber, and went wheel-to-wheel with Kush Maini through the Nouvelle Chicane. Both cut the kerbs; Beganovic's warmer tyres took the place. Maini held off Stenshorne for P4 by a few hundredths.

Tsolov was unchallenged out front. He crossed the line 9.013 seconds clear, set the Feature fastest lap (1:22.244), and banked 26 points — 25 for the win plus the 1-point fastest-lap bonus.

Win in Monaco again, third win in a row, I could not feel any better. We are again in the championship fight, so heading into Barcelona I am feeling quite confident. — Nikola Tsolov, post-race

The Minì collapse

The other half of the 21-point swing happened in the pit lane.

Minì qualified Group A P4 and lined up P6 on the Feature grid. He drove a clean Sprint to P3 (6 points) — a disciplined, percentage drive that, in his own words after Saturday, was "never a plan to try and overtake" once track position was set.

The Feature plan came apart on Lap 26. By Lap 22 his Soft tyres were showing severe wear banding and he'd dropped three seconds behind Beganovic. MP Motorsport called him in to fit the Supersofts. The crew cross-threaded a front-axle wheel nut. The stop took long enough to drop him from P5 to P17.

It got worse on the out-lap. Sebastián Montoya, on the alternative strategy with warm Softs from his early Lap 8 stop, was flying. He swept around the outside of Minì at Massenet, then went past Bilinski at Mirabeau on the same lap. Stuck behind the train Montoya had just cleared, Minì could only salvage P11. Zero Feature points.

The Italian was philosophical:

We were quick in practice, but we missed a bit in qualifying. I'm still quite happy with my performance and the team performance. If you're fast enough to arrive P8, you arrive P8, and if you're fast enough to win, you try and win. — Gabriele Minì, post-race

The math is less philosophical. Minì led the championship by 22 points on Friday night. By Sunday evening it was one.

The Campos takeover nobody is talking about

The driver story will dominate the headlines, but the team story has bigger long-run implications. Campos Racing left Monaco leading the Teams' Championship — a Sprint win for León and a Feature win for Tsolov on the same weekend.

This matters because F2's Teams' Championship is not just a vanity title. It determines paddock allocation, garage position, and FIA prize money — all of which feed back into operational quality next season. Campos has now won at every circuit visited in 2026 — Melbourne (Tsolov Feature), Miami (Tsolov Sprint), Montréal (no win but consistent points), and Monaco (both races). That kind of record makes the team a serious option for the next generation of junior drivers looking for an F2 seat.

Campos team principal Adrián Campos Jr. captured the timing:

We are entering a crucial phase of the season as the championship arrives in Europe. We have won at every circuit visited so far this season, so we arrive in Monaco with confidence. We will give everything to leave Monaco satisfied with a job well done. — Adrián Campos Jr., Campos Racing

They did.

The Red Bull dimension

Tsolov is the only Red Bull Junior on the 2026 F2 grid. The Campos #6 he drives is the seat last held by Isack Hadjar and Arvid Lindblad — both now on the Racing Bulls F1 grid. That lineage is not a coincidence. Campos is a contracted Red Bull pipeline team, and the #6 is where the program puts its strongest junior.

What that pressure actually feels like came out earlier this season, when Tsolov revealed that Helmut Marko had called him in Abu Dhabi at the end of 2025 — on the eve of Marko's official Red Bull retirement — with a message that the formal organisational change wasn't changing anything else:

Everything is staying the same way. You still have to perform. You still have to win races, and that's the number one thing. — Helmut Marko, relayed by Tsolov

Marko is officially retired. He still texts Tsolov congratulations after wins. There is no formal seat decision pending until late in the season, and the immediate F1 graduates from this Campos seat have both needed F2 podium consistency to get there. Tsolov is, after Monaco, the only rookie in F2 history to win in his first Monaco F2 weekend. The Red Bull pipeline is watching.

If you want the deeper read on what this Monaco result actually does to his 2027 odds, the F1 seat analysis is the place. The short version: a Feature win at Monaco moves the needle on every factor in the model except Tsunoda's contract.

What changes for Barcelona

The Honest Verdict

The title fight is a fight again. Both drivers know it.

Going into Monaco, Minì's 22-point cushion was psychological as much as mathematical — a buffer big enough to absorb a bad weekend. He had one. He could not absorb it. The cushion is gone. Barcelona is now the first round where both championship contenders arrive under genuine pressure to score.

Tsolov's three wins, all from the front of the grid, suggest a driver who is at his strongest when the car has clean air and qualifying has gone right. Barcelona qualifies differently — single-group, harder rear tyre, more abrasive surface — and Tsolov has never raced an F2 car there. Minì has. That is the first variable to watch on Friday afternoon.

The Câmara consistency story is over. The Tsolov–Minì duel is the story now.

Frequently asked questions

Who won the F2 Monaco 2026 Feature Race?

Nikola Tsolov (Campos Racing) won the Monaco Feature Race on 7 June 2026, taking pole position and fastest lap on the way to a 9.013-second victory over Alex Dunne (Rodin Motorsport). Dino Beganovic (DAMS) completed the podium after a final-lap pass on Kush Maini through the Nouvelle Chicane.

How did Tsolov go from P3 to P2 in the F2 championship at Monaco?

Tsolov scored 27 points across the Monaco weekend — 1 point from Sprint Race fastest lap and 26 points from the Feature Race (25 for the win plus 1 for fastest lap). Championship leader Gabriele Minì scored just 6 across the weekend (Sprint P3, Feature P11 after a pit-stop problem). The 21-point swing took Tsolov from P3, 22 points behind, to P2, one point behind.

What happened to Rafael Câmara at Monaco?

Câmara took overall pole on Friday and led the early stages of Sunday's Feature Race. The race-defining moment came on Lap 35: after both Câmara and Tsolov pitted within a lap of each other, Câmara emerged with cold Supersoft tyres while Tsolov already had a flying lap on warmed rubber. Under DRS pressure into Turn 1, Câmara locked up, ran across the kerbs, and crashed into the Sainte Dévote barriers. He did not finish. F2 prohibits tyre warmers, which made the out-lap traction deficit unavoidable.

What happened to Gabriele Minì's pit stop at Monaco?

MP Motorsport called Minì in for fresh Supersoft tyres on Lap 26. A cross-threaded front-axle wheel nut caused a long delay, dropping him from P5 to P17 on exit. He recovered to P11 by the flag but scored zero Feature points. Combined with his Sprint P3 (6 points), Minì left Monaco with only 6 for the weekend and saw his championship lead cut from 22 to 1.

What is the F2 championship situation after Monaco 2026?

Minì leads on 63 points, Tsolov P2 on 62 (gap: 1). Stenshorne and Dunne are level on 48, with Stenshorne ahead on countback wins. León is P5 on 45. Campos Racing leads the Teams' Championship on 107 points after the Sprint win for León and the Feature win for Tsolov on the same weekend — an 11-point margin over Rodin Motorsport. Ten rounds remain in the season.
